Bézout's identity, also known as Bézout's lemma, states that for any two integers `a` and `b` with a greatest common divisor `d`, there exist integers `x` and `y` such that `ax + by = d`. F[1]urthermore, all integers of the form `az + bt` are exactly the multiples of `d`. T[1]he integers `x` and `y` are called Bézout coefficients and are not unique. Th[1]ey can be computed using the extended Euclidean algorithm. F[1]or example, the greatest common divisor of 15 and 69 is 3, and this can be expressed as `3 = 15 × (−9) + 69 × 2`, where -9 and 2 are the Bézout coefficients. Bézout[1]'s identity can be generalized to more than two integers and also applies to univariate polynomials over a field. It also [1]holds true in any principal ideal domain.
