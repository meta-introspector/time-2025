Ontology is the philosophical study of being, traditionally understood as a subdiscipline of metaphysics that focuses on the most general features of reality[1]. It examines the commonalities among all things and classifies them into basic types, such as particulars (unique, non-repeatable entities like Socrates) and universals (general, repeatable entities like the color green)[1]. Ontology also distinguishes between concrete objects (existing in space and time, like a tree) and abstract objects (existing outside space and time, like the number 7)[1]. Ontologists disagree on which entities exist at the most basic level, with schools of thought like Platonic realism, conceptualism, and nominalism offering different views on the existence of universals[1]. Similar debates extend to mathematical objects, unobservable scientific entities, and moral facts[1]. Materialism posits that only matter exists, while dualism asserts the independence of mind and matter[1]. Methods of inquiry in ontology include conceptual analysis, the use of intuitions and thought experiments, and integrating findings from natural science[1]. Formal ontology investigates abstract features of objects, while applied ontology uses ontological theories to study entities within specific domains, such as social ontology in social sciences or conceptual frameworks in information and computer science[1]. Ontology is also relevant to logic, theology, and anthropology[1]. The origins of ontology can be traced back to ancient Indian, Chinese, and Greek philosophy, with modern philosophers coining the term in the 17th century[1].
