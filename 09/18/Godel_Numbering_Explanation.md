## Gödel Numbering and "Kurt Gödel as a Number"

It's a fascinating observation, and it leads directly into the profound implications of Kurt Gödel's work!

When we talk about "capturing Kurt Gödel as a number" or "his number," we're touching upon the core idea behind **Gödel numbering**.

**What is Gödel Numbering?**
Kurt Gödel, in his incompleteness theorems, devised a system to assign a unique natural number (a **Gödel number**) to each symbol, formula, and even entire sequences of formulas (like proofs) within a formal mathematical system. This process is analogous to how a computer encodes text into binary numbers.

**Kurt Gödel as a Number?**
It's important to clarify that Gödel numbering is applied to *statements* and *proofs* within a formal system, not directly to people. So, there isn't a single, inherent "Gödel number" for Kurt Gödel the person.

However, one could hypothetically:
1.  Assign a Gödel number to a **formal statement about Kurt Gödel** (e.g., "Kurt Gödel proved the incompleteness theorems").
2.  Assign a Gödel number to the **text of one of his papers** or even his biography, treated as a sequence of symbols in a formal language.

**What is "His Number"?**
There isn't *one* specific number that is "his number." The actual Gödel number would depend entirely on:
*   **The specific Gödel numbering scheme used:** There are many ways to construct a Gödel numbering, and each would yield a different number for the same statement.
*   **The exact formal statement being encoded:** A slight change in wording or symbols would result in a different Gödel number.

**Connection to "Fixed Point of Terms":**
Your observation about capturing ideas with numbers and the concept of a "fixed point of terms" is highly insightful. Gödel's work demonstrates how formal systems can achieve a form of self-reference. By assigning numbers to statements, he showed that statements could effectively "talk about themselves" or "talk about their own provability" within the system. This self-referential capacity is what allowed him to construct statements that are true but unprovable within the system, leading to the incompleteness theorems.

So, while Kurt Gödel doesn't have a single "number" in the way a social security number identifies a person, his legacy is deeply intertwined with the idea of assigning numbers to abstract concepts to explore the fundamental limits of formal systems and knowledge itself.
