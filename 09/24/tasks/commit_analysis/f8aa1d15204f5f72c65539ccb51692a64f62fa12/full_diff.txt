+# Glossary
+
+This document provides definitions for the key terms used in our tutorials.
+
+---
+
+*   **Bézout's identity:** A theorem about the relationship between the greatest common divisor of two numbers and a linear combination of those numbers.
+*   **centered hexagonal number:** A hexagonal number with a dot in the center and all other dots surrounding the center in a hexagonal lattice.
+*   **centered triangular number:** A triangular number with a dot in the center and all other dots surrounding the center in a triangular lattice.
+*   **coprime:** Two integers are coprime if their only common positive divisor is 1.
+*   **Euclidean algorithm:** An efficient method for computing the greatest common divisor (GCD) of two integers.
+*   **Fermat prime:** A prime number that is of the form 2^(2^n) + 1.
+*   **Griess algebra:** A 196,883-dimensional commutative non-associative algebra used to construct the Monster group.
+*   **heptadecagon:** A 17-sided polygon.
+*   **Keith number:** A number n that appears in a sequence starting with the digits of n and where each subsequent term is the sum of the previous k terms, where k is the number of digits of n.
+*   **Mathieu group M₁₁:** The smallest of the 26 sporadic simple groups.
+*   **Metonic cycle:** A period of 19 years that is used to reconcile solar and lunar calendars.
+*   **Monster group:** The largest of the 26 sporadic simple groups.
+*   **monstrous moonshine:** A surprising connection between the Monster group and modular functions.
+*   **Riemann zeta function:** A function of a complex variable that is important in number theory.
+*   **sharply 4-transitive:** A property of a group that means that for any two ordered sets of four distinct elements, there is exactly one element in the group that maps the first set to the second.
+*   **sporadic simple group:** One of the 26 exceptional groups in the classification of finite simple groups that do not belong to any infinite family.
+*   **Steiner system:** A type of block design, which is a set of points and a collection of subsets of those points (called blocks).
+*   **vertex operator algebra:** An algebraic structure that plays an important role in conformal field theory and string theory.

+#!/usr/bin/env bash
+
+# Get the list of cached HTML files
+HTML_FILES=$(ls wikipedia_cache/*.html)
+
+NEW_URLS=()
+
+for file in $HTML_FILES; do
+    # Extract /wiki/ links from the HTML content
+    # Filter out links containing ':' (e.g., File:, Category:) and anchors (#)
+    # Prepend the base Wikipedia URL
+    # Use sort -u to get unique URLs
+    extracted_links=$(cat "$file" | \
+        grep -oE 'href="/wiki/[^"#:]+"' | \
+        sed -E 's/href="//;s/"$//' | \
+        sed -E 's/^/https:\/\/en.wikipedia.org/' | \
+        sort -u)
+
+    # Add to NEW_URLS array
+    while IFS= read -r line; do
+        NEW_URLS+=("$line")
+    done <<< "$extracted_links"
+done
+
+# Get unique URLs from the collected list
+UNIQUE_NEW_URLS=$(printf "%s\n" "${NEW_URLS[@]}" | sort -u)
+
+# Output the unique new URLs
+echo "$UNIQUE_NEW_NEW_URLS"

+Bézout's identity, also known as Bézout's lemma, states that for any two integers `a` and `b` with a greatest common divisor `d`, there exist integers `x` and `y` such that `ax + by = d`. F[1]urthermore, all integers of the form `az + bt` are exactly the multiples of `d`. T[1]he integers `x` and `y` are called Bézout coefficients and are not unique. Th[1]ey can be computed using the extended Euclidean algorithm.
+
+F[1]or example, the greatest common divisor of 15 and 69 is 3, and this can be expressed as `3 = 15 × (−9) + 69 × 2`, where -9 and 2 are the Bézout coefficients.
+
+Bézout[1]'s identity can be generalized to more than two integers and also applies to univariate polynomials over a field. It also [1]holds true in any principal ideal domain.
+
+Histori[1]cally, while Étienne Bézout proved this identity for polynomials, the statement for integers was found earlier in the work of Claude Gaspard Bachet de Méziriac.[1]

+Ontology is the philosophical study of being, traditionally understood as a subdiscipline of metaphysics that focuses on the most general features of reality[1]. It examines the commonalities among all things and classifies them into basic types, such as particulars (unique, non-repeatable entities like Socrates) and universals (general, repeatable entities like the color green)[1]. Ontology also distinguishes between concrete objects (existing in space and time, like a tree) and abstract objects (existing outside space and time, like the number 7)[1].
+
+Ontologists disagree on which entities exist at the most basic level, with schools of thought like Platonic realism, conceptualism, and nominalism offering different views on the existence of universals[1]. Similar debates extend to mathematical objects, unobservable scientific entities, and moral facts[1]. Materialism posits that only matter exists, while dualism asserts the independence of mind and matter[1].
+
+Methods of inquiry in ontology include conceptual analysis, the use of intuitions and thought experiments, and integrating findings from natural science[1]. Formal ontology investigates abstract features of objects, while applied ontology uses ontological theories to study entities within specific domains, such as social ontology in social sciences or conceptual frameworks in information and computer science[1]. Ontology is also relevant to logic, theology, and anthropology[1].
+
+The origins of ontology can be traced back to ancient Indian, Chinese, and Greek philosophy, with modern philosophers coining the term in the 17th century[1].
