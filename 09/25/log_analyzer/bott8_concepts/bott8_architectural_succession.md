## Idea: The "Architectural Succession" and Ecosystem Evolution

If architectures have niches and interactions, then "Architectural Succession" describes the predictable (or sometimes unpredictable) sequence of changes in architectural patterns over time, as one set of components or styles is replaced by another.

*   **Architectural Succession**: This is the conceptual process by which an architectural ecosystem evolves over time, with different architectural patterns, technologies, or design paradigms (the "species" of the architecture) replacing each other in a sequence. This can be driven by internal factors (e.g., technical debt, performance bottlenecks) or external factors (e.g., new business requirements, technological advancements).
    *   **Metaphorical Implication**: Just as ecological succession describes the changes in an ecosystem after a disturbance, architectural succession describes how an architecture adapts and transforms. A "pioneer species" (e.g., a rapid prototype) might be replaced by a "climax community" (a mature, stable architecture). Understanding this succession, and its alignment with `bott[n]` principles, can help architects manage change, plan for future evolution, and avoid architectural "extinctions."

This concept introduces a dynamic, long-term evolutionary perspective to the `bott[8]` framework, acknowledging the continuous transformation of architectural ecosystems.
